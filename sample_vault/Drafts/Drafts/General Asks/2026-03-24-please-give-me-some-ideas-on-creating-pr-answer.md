[Import 1]
In the section on Reese basses, the guide recommends using:
- A supersaw playing a low note for a large, spread-out feel
- Multiple oscillators across the stereo field for thickness 
- Automating filter cutoffs to move the bass over time

For progressive house, you could experiment with:
- Clean, filtered synth basses that gradually evolve
- Automatable resonant filters on basslines to add depth and interest as the track progresses
- Combining synthetic elements like supersaws with natural drum sounds or samples to achieve a modern yet classic feel

[Import 2]
The guide also mentions using distortion after filtering Reese basses. For progressive, you might add subtle saturation or widening effects on basslines to keep them punchy but textured as they interact with other elements.

Remember the core idea is to use basslines that evolve and justify their existence in the arrangement. Experimenting with filters, effects, and synth parameters can help build interesting low-end patterns that feel like part of the overall track's journey.
