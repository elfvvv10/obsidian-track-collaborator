Module 4 Mixing & Details Guide

This guide will 1) Suggest what to check when mixing your track, 2) Manage expectations for where your track should be in the production process, and 3) Give you some tests to check everything is working.

Goals:

Your track has appropriate details & ear candy added.

Your track is mixed well - ready for mastering.

Managing Expectations for Mixing:

Your frequency spread won’t look identical to your reference track, because:

They are different tracks with different composition and sounds and

Yours hasn’t been mastered yet.

This is 100% normal and FINE. However - you want to be close - so the low end should be hitting about the same level on your spectrum analyser, and the high end should look similar, too. There will be more variation in the mids, but you can still use it to compare, for example, you don’t want to have 6dB spikes or dips in comparison to your reference track.

Your reference track probably has 10-20 years of production experience behind it, and has been professionally mixed and mastered. So, at this point you are comparing:

Relative levels

Clarity (EQ)

Reverb and delay amounts

Stereo width (less important)

Ignore trying to match the loudness at this stage. That will happen in the mastering module.

If you have set your reference track to -12 peak (or, ideally, -18 LUFS), and you’ve followed the basic starting levels, you should be set-up and ready to mix.

Common Mixing Mistakes to be Aware Of:

Processing individual sounds too much, which then get processed at the group and mastering stages (cumulative).

Not being mindful of gain staging.

Not being mindful of the “waterfall effect” (e.g. group compression). This is why good sounds balanced well is the best place to start.

Applying processing to one sound just because it sounds good on another.

Not doing the visual A/B checks with the meters against reference track.

Not checking the mix frequently in mono.

Important: Your master channel, buses and group faders should still be left at 0 for this entire mixing process. This is because if you (for example) set your kick to peak at -12dB, but you’ve put the kick & snare fader, or drum bus fader down to -3dB, your kick drum is actually peaking at -15 (-12 -3), which will throw off all of the suggested starting levels.

Useful Tools:

When going through this process, it’s definitely worth using your mono-switch regularly, so it’s easier to focus on energy levels and frequencies (rather than the stereo field).Closing your eyes or looking away from your computer monitor can also help focus your ears.

Stem splitting can still be useful at this stage of the process - but less so than in earlier stages. You can use it to try and hear the kind of reverb amounts that would be suitable on your instruments. However, that would still only be a group stem - not each individual instrument - so its use is limited.

Important Note:

How good a sound sounds only matters in the context of the mix, so - once you’ve completed the Corrective “black box” (which is usually easier when a sound is solo’ed), make sure all other moves are checked in context. Working in hierarchical order is the way to avoid getting lost.

The Black Box Processing Chain Overview:

Green: Always want / Orange: Likely want / Red: Totally dependent on sound and intention (not good or bad).

Corrective (Tuning / Timing / Gain-Staging) - Prepping sound / sound design.

Colouration (Saturation / Bit-Crushers / Distortion / OTT / Phasing / Flanging / Chorus) - Adding harmonics.

Reductive (Reductive EQ) - Removing unwanted frequencies.

Dynamic (Compression) - Controlling dynamics. If in doubt, compress less!

Movement & Automation (LP&HP Sweeps / Reverb Washouts / Volume Automation)

Mix Channel (Stereo field / Final Shaping EQ)

Auxiliary Spatial (Aux channels reverb/delay)

Bus or Group (Colour, EQ, Dynamics)

Now let’s go through each stage of the details and mixing process:

1. Adding FX:

Looping the appropriate part of your track, bring up your FX from silent until it does the job and brings the energy it needs to without overbearing the mix. A/B check against your reference track.

2. Setting Individual Channel Levels:

The basic mix levels should already be used from Module 1, but, as a recap:

Notes:

The suggested levels are cumulative.

There is only so much you can pre-plan levels, as it’s dependent on genre, sounds and specific track.

The most important are the master channel target, the kick and sub bass levels (dependable, regardless of genre). Once these are set, your referencing spectrum analyser is the most useful tool.

Master Channel Target:

Master Channel: Peaking between -4dB and -8dB when channel fader is left at 0.

Suggest Channel Levels in Hierarchy (Please note whether Peak or RMS):

Kick: Peaking at -12dB

Sub Bass (under 100Hz): Averaging -20dB RMS

Mid Bass(s) (above 100Hz): Bring up to balance with kick and sub (totally depends on bass sound as to suggested level, so do this by comparing to your reference track)

Main Clap/Snare: Peaking about -12dB

Main Hat (in between each kick): Peaking about -15 to -16dB

Percussion (everything that’s not kick, main clap/snare, main hat, crashes): Averaging between -30 and -36dB RMS

Processed Vocals: Averaging -24dB (±2 dB) / Peaking about -12 (±2 dB)

All the other elements should be brought up in order of importance.

Quick check before moving on to step 3:

Does my track have a similar frequency spread to my reference track at the business part of the track?

3. Stereo width:

Tools we can use to create stereo width:

Unison/chorus

Layering

Haas Effect

Autopanning

Ping pong delay

Reverb

Panning

Ozone Stereo Imager (Polyverse Widener is good, too)

Quick check before moving on to step 4:

Keep in mind the stereo field “teeter totter” or see-saw. Is everything balanced?

4. Auxiliary Channel Reverbs:

Set up your global room reverb if you haven’t already, with 100% wet and a decay of under 500ms.

Create a global hall reverb if needs be.

Have a dedicated reverb for your main lead / instrument / vocal.

Make sure there is no reverb under 120Hz.

Quick check before moving on to step 5:

Does my track sound better or worse when each reverb aux channel is muted?

Does my track suddenly become muddy when the reverb is on? If so, the reverb might be too loud, or it might need to have some reductive EQ applied to it (carving out a little space - nothing extreme or too narrow in term of Q settings).

5. Group Processing:

Recommended to start with the drum group when it comes to processing.

Moves should usually be quite subtle at the group processing stage. However, if processing it hard sounds good, it sounds good!

Just be aware of what’s happening at every stage of the chain, and have an intention, and above all - gain stage so you can accurately judge whether you prefer the change in tone and dynamics (rather than just an increase in volume). Try and match the peak volume on your channel fader before and after each plugin.

Quick check before moving on to the self-tests:

When I turn each entire group chain on (in context of the entire mix), does it sound better and suitable for my genre when I compare it to my reference track?

Is the peak level of my group fader no more than 1 dB different than before the processing?

Self-Tests:

Hint: For all these tests, A) switching to mono regularly will really help your ears focus on energy, frequencies and whether masking or phase cancellation is occurring, and B) either closing your eyes or looking away from your computer monitor can help focus your ears.

These are all also intended to be done at the highest energy part of your track (usually the final drop or post-drop), unless otherwise stated.

Top-tip: Usually, the third and fourth bar of the drop or post drop are good places to loop to check the mix, so the cymbals, crashes or white noise don’t interfere (which hit in the first bar and sometimes take a while to die out). Something to be mindful of.

Visual Check:

Does your final drop/post drop frequency spread look similar to your reference track? If not, try and determine where the potential discrepancies lie, and why.

Low-End Comparison Test:

Use your low-pass referencing roll-off on your reference track at 100Hz, and set your master channel referencing roll-off at the same place.

Solo-ing your kick drum, does the peak look similar on your spectrum analyser compared to your reference track?

Does the low-end energy sound similar? Is it as punchy, and the correct length?

Then, turn on your bass, too, and do the same comparison.

Is your low-end energy looking similar and sounding similar to your reference track?

Is there space and clarity between each sub-bass hit?

If not - this needs fixing before moving on.

If trying out new kick drums, make sure to set to peak at -12, and turn off any drum bus processing (to avoid the waterfall effect), then check in the same way against your reference track.

Then make sure your sub bass is averaging about -20RMS, and double check the ADSR settings are suitable for your sub bass (particularly decay and release, so they aren’t too long). You can bring it up from silent with the referencing roll-offs still activated, and check against your reference track.

Low-Volume Mixing Test:

Turn your monitoring down to be very quiet, and listen to your track.

Can you still clearly hear the kick, bass, main clap/snare position and main lead synth/instrument//vocal?

Do they still sound balanced?

Is anything jumping out aggressively?

Are any of your core (important) sounds disappearing completely?

Compare against your reference track at this low volume, too. Doing this in mono can really help.

Mud Troubleshooting:

Quick mix health checks:

If you have mud in your mix or a lack of clarity, check/do the following:

Are your basic starting levels correct, and your master channel peaking between -4 and -8dB?

Is your bass treatment correct (ideal sub bass range and level, EQ roll-offs for sub-bass and mid-basses).

Is the low-end of your other elements rolled off so they aren’t fighting with your sub 100Hz kick and bass?

Is your sidechain bounce under 100Hz set to 100%? Is your sidechain bounce above 100Hz suitable for your genre?

Are all your auxiliary channels high-pass rolled-off at at least 120Hz?

Does your frequency spread look similar to your reference track?

Steps to find the mud (once all those checks are done):

Mute everything.

Then, unmute your kick, and compare to reference track. Sounding good? If not, change kick, then proceed.

Unmute your sub bass. Sounding good with the kick? No? Fix. Yes? Proceed.

Unmute your main lead/vocal. Sounding good with kick and bass? No? Identify what’s clashing and fix. Yes? Proceed.

Then continue in this fashion until you unmute the instrument where the mix suddenly sounds muddy. That is the sound to focus on fixing - probably with EQ so it doesn’t clash with your core elements further up in the hierarchy.

No FX Energy Test:Turn off all your FX sounds (crashes and sweeps, etc.) and check your drop still hits effectively after your build. If yes, fantastic! If not, try and determine why not and fix before re-adding the FX.

Section Levels Comparison Test:The differential in levels between your drops to breaks should still be in the right ballpark. Usually, the differential of peak volume is roughly 6dB (assuming no kick playing in the breaks). E.g. if your drop / post drop is peaking between -4 to -8dB (with the master channel left at 0), the break might be peaking between -10 to 14dB.

If there’s no difference – or a huge difference – check your basic starting levels (-12 Kick, -20RMS sub bass, etc.).

Individual Channels and Bus/Group Levels Gain-Staging Test:When you bypass any processing plugin (either individual plugins or a whole processing chain), - at either the individual channel or group level - is the peak level on the channel fader within 1 or 2 dB of when the plugins are activated? If there is a greater than 2dB difference, your ears will likely be focussing mostly on the change in volume, rather than purely on the colour or dynamics of the sound.

Stereo Field Test:Are your core elements balanced centrally on the stereo “teeter-totter” or see-saw, i.e.

Kick

Main clap/snare

Main hat position

16th groove hats

Mid Bass

Sub bass (should be mono anyway and in the centre)

Vocals / Lead

Pads

Secondly - do they sound balanced both in mono and in stereo?

Thirdly, comparing with a vector scope against your reference track, does it look similar? If not, try and determine why not. Note: It’s not hugely important, as we can add some more stereo width at the mastering stage.

Visual Arrangement Test

When you have exported your waveform, does your waveform visually show:

Clear section changes

Density increases toward drops

Reduced density in breakdowns

If the waveform looks flat - or the breaks and builds look as dense as the drop - the arrangement likely lacks contrast.

Final Comparison:

Switch between the reference track followed immediately by your track, and ask yourself three questions:

Does my main drop have a similar frequency spread to my reference track?

Is my master channel peak level between -4 and -6 dB peak?

Is my master channel average LUFS level between -12 and -17 LUFS?

If the answer to all three is yes, your arrangement is complete, and ready to submit. Congratulations….this is one of the hardest parts of production!
